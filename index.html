How does the autonomous vehicle industry prove that their technology is safe?

By Emily Yip

Autonomous vehicles (AV) are transforming modern transportation. As AV companies expand into various metropolitan cities such as San Francisco, Las Vegas, and Phoenix, concerns regarding passenger safety continue to shape public perception. Understanding how AV companies demonstrate that their vehicles are safe is critical to gaining public trust and successful widespread deployment. This document provides an overview of the methods and data that AV companies employ to communicate the safety of their vehicles.

National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) Framework for Automated Driving Safety (ADS) A framework that outlines how AV developers should design, test, and validate their vehicles.

ISO 26262 international standard for functional safety in road vehicles point.

ISO 21448 (SOTIF - Safety of the Intended Functionality) Addresses safety risks that arise from a system functioning as intended, but acts in a way that is unsafe or unexpected in specific situations

UL 4600 international safety standard for autonomous vehicles

Safety Metrics

AV companies evaluate their cars on these metrics and compare them to human crash benchmarks to highlight that autonomy can actually be safer than human driving. The human crash benchmarks are public data published by the NHTSA.

Beyond the Data

One of the most effective methods in building the credibility of autonomous vehicles is community outreach. From someone who has been in an AV before, describing my positive experience to my family and friends transforms their safety concerns into excitement for the future of autonomous technology.

Zoox does community outreach by holding hands-on first responder training workshops to help them identify emergency safety functions and address concerns (Zoox, 2025a). Zoox also includes testimonial messages from members of the police and fire departments from its operating cities in its safety report.

Waymo also has its own Community Partners division, where they collaborate with organizations both local to San Francisco and nation-wide to spread awareness on the accessibility and safety of their vehicles (Waymo, 2025). Waymo has partnered with local San Francisco nonprofits such as the Lighthouse for the Blind SF and The Epilepsy Foundation to demonstrate that their vehicles as a safe transportation option for disabled communities. To spark interest in AV in the broader population, Waymo also sponsors content creators such as Veritasium to document their experience of riding in a Waymo vehicle.
