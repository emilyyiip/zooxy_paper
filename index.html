How does the autonomous vehicle industry show that their technology is safe?

By Emily Yip

Autonomous vehicles (AV) are transforming modern transportation. As AV companies expand into various metropolitan cities such as San Francisco, Las Vegas, and Phoenix, concerns regarding passenger safety continue to shape public perception. Understanding how AV companies demonstrate that their vehicles are safe is critical to gaining public trust and successful widespread deployment. This document provides an overview of the methods and data that AV companies employ to communicate the safety of their vehicles.

AV companies adhere to the following standards for the development of their vehicles:

National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) Framework for Automated Driving Safety (ADS) A framework that outlines how AV developers should design, test, and validate their vehicles.

ISO 26262 International standard for functional safety in road vehicles point.

ISO 21448 (SOTIF - Safety of the Intended Functionality) Addresses safety risks that arise from a system functioning as intended, but acts in a way that is unsafe or unexpected in specific situations

UL 4600 international safety standard for autonomous vehicles

Kooper, Kerrell, Fratnik, 2019

These standards establish baseline safety standards for the production and functions of autonomous vehicles. They ensure that the vehicles have consistent system performance, which provides transparency and builds public confidence.

Data such as injury causing crashes and vehicle to vehicle collisions tell how often crashes occur and their severity, showing that their vehicles reduce the chances of life-threatening events. Delta-V and incidents per million miles provide operational insight into how their vehicles anticipate hazards and handle complex scenarios. These metrics combined indicate whether an autonomous system is actually safer than a human driver, how it behaves in risky environments, and where it could improve in performance, building transparency and public trust.

Test and Simulation Data

AV Companies rigorously test their vehicles through simulations that recreate high risk edge cases using sensors, labelled objects, and highly detailed mapping.

Sensors

AV companies use light and detection ranging data (LiDAR) to measure depth from a vehicle to objects, enabling the creation of a detailed 3D mapping of its surroundings.

Vehicles also use radars for real-time speed and distance measurements. Radars excel in harsher weather and lighting conditions.

Long wave and infared cameras are used to detect pedestrians in low light and provide visibility through fog and smoke.

Objects

Extensively labelled object datasets are needed to simulate realistic scenarios. Companies use various 3D and 2D semantic segmentation labels, video camera data, and features per pixel ray information. They include images ranging from pedestrians and bicyclists to road signs and vegetation, ensuring their models are generalizable to all roads.

Mapping

Detailed mapping of roads in operating cities is critical to AV performance. AV companies create 3D maps of road graph segments, which includes boundaries, road signs, bumps, and entrances to driveways. These maps also store semantic information such as which lanes pedestrians will typically cross in and traffic flows on busier intersections.

With this data, companies can conduct large-scale simulations and road testing to show the safety of their vehicles. To make these results more transparent, they also share footage of their vehicles navigating challenging traffic scenarios. For instance, Aurora uploads videos of their truck navigating aggressive swerves and tire blowouts on their website, while Waymo posts videos of their vehicle navigating road occlusions and sudden pedestrian appearances on their Youtube channel.

Safety Innovations

AV companies show the safety of their vehicles by highlighting safety innovation features. Zoox emphasizes their bidirectional vehicle design, a four wheel independent steering, braking system, seatbelt crash avoidance system, and independent redundant backup systems on their website . Zoox also includes a Missions Operations Team that monitors the health of the robotaxis real time, ensuring safety during the ride.

Beyond the Data

One of the most effective methods in building the credibility of autonomous vehicles is community outreach. As someone who has been in an AV before, describing my positive experience to my family and friends immediately turns their safety concerns into enthusiasm for the future of autonomous technology.

Zoox
First responder training workshops are held to train workers to identify emergency safety functions in the vehicle. Zoox also includes testimonial messages from members of the police and fire departments from its operating cities.

Waymo
The Comunity Partners division collaborates with organizations both local to San Francisco and nationwide, such as Lighthouse for the Blind SF and The Epilepsy Foundation to spread awareness on the accessibility and safety of their vehicles for the disabled (Waymo, 2025). To spark interest in AV in the broader population, Waymo also sponsors content creators to document their experience of riding in a Waymo vehicle for the first time.
