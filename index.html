How does the autonomous vehicle industry show that their vehicles are safe?

By Emily Yip

Autonomous vehicles (AV) are transforming modern transportation. As AV companies expand into various metropolitan cities such as San Francisco, Las Vegas, and Phoenix, concerns regarding passenger safety continue to shape public perception. Understanding how AV companies demonstrate that their vehicles are safe is critical to gaining public trust and successful widespread deployment. This document provides an overview of the methods and data that AV companies employ to communicate the safety of their vehicles.

AV companies adhere to the following standards for the development of their vehicles:

National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) Framework for Automated Driving Safety (ADS) Outlines how AV developers should design, test, and validate their vehicles.

ISO 26262 International standard for functional safety in road vehicles.

ISO 21448 (SOTIF - Safety of the Intended Functionality) Addresses safety risks that arise from a system functioning as intended, but acts in a way that is unsafe or unexpected in specific situations.

UL 4600 International safety standard for autonomous vehicles.

Koopman et al., 2019

These standards establish a foundation of baseline safety that guides the production and functionality of AVs. They ensure that the vehicles have consistent system performance, which provides transparency and builds public confidence.

Safety Metric Data

AV companies evaluate their vehicles on the following metrics to quantify their performance compared to human crash benchmarks. The human crash benchmarks are public data published by the NHTSA.

By using these data metrics, prospective riders can understand how often crashes occur in AVs and their severity. This quantification allows them to determine whether riding in an AV is actually safer than a human driver and where it could improve in performance, providing further transparency.

Test and Simulation Data

AV companies rigorously test the perception and localization ability of their vehicles through simulations that recreate edge cases to show that they can navigate high-risk scenarios. Conducting and evaluating vehicle performance in these simulations require sensor data, labelled objects, and highly detailed mapping.

Sensors

AV companies use light and detection ranging data (LiDAR) to measure depth from a vehicle to objects, enabling the creation of a detailed 3D mapping of its surroundings.

Vehicles also use radars for real-time speed and distance measurements. Radars excel in harsher weather and lighting conditions.

LiDAR and radars are combined to detect incoming vehicles and their speeds, park, perform lane changes, and other vehicle functions.

Objects

Extensively labelled object datasets are needed to simulate realistic scenarios and train computer vision models. Companies use various 3D and 2D semantic segmentations, bounding box labels on camera and LiDAR data, and key point labels on object frames. They include objects ranging from pedestrians and bicyclists to road signs and vegetation, ensuring their models are generalizable to all roads.

Mapping

Detailed mapping of roads in operating cities is critical to AV testing. AV companies create 3D maps of road segments, which includes boundaries, road signs, bumps, and entrances to driveways. These maps also store semantic information such as which lanes pedestrians will typically cross in and traffic flows on busier intersections, allowing vehicles to learn more complex patterns in the data.

Ondruš et al.,2019; Waymo, 2024; Zoox, 2018

To communicate these results, AV companies share footage of their vehicles navigating challenging traffic scenarios. Aurora uploads videos of their truck navigating aggressive swerves and tire blowouts on their website, while Waymo posts videos of their vehicle navigating road occlusions and sudden pedestrian appearances on their Youtube channel.

Beyond the Data

One of the most effective methods in building the credibility of AVs is community outreach. As someone who has been in an autonomous vehicle before, describing my positive experience to my family and friends usually turns their safety concerns into enthusiasm for the future of autonomous technology.

Zoox
Workshops are held to educate first responders in identifying emergency safety functions in the vehicle. Zoox also published an emergency response guide and rescue sheet, which details how to properly interact with the vehicle in the event of an emergency. To strengthen credibility, Zoox includes testimonial messages from members of the police and fire departments in its operating cities.

Waymo
The Community Partners division collaborates with organizations both local to San Francisco and nationwide, such as Lighthouse for the Blind SF and The Epilepsy Foundation to spread awareness on the accessibility and safety of their vehicles for the disabled. To spark interest in AV in the broader population, Waymo also sponsors content creators to document their experience of riding in a Waymo vehicle for the first time.

Conclusion

AV companies use various data metrics to communicate the safety of their vehicles, such as crash data and high-risk simulation testing results, as well as community outreach. As these companies continue to expand into increasingly complex environments, the importance of data-driven storytelling and transparency will only grow. Demonstrating accountability for safety is critical to gaining public trust in autonomous technology and ultimately ensuring its long term success.
